If your sleep is out of phase with your life, the first thing to know is that willpower probably isn&apos;t the problem. Your circadian rhythm — the roughly 24-hour internal clock in your brain — responds to environmental cues, especially light. Changing those cues is often more effective than &quot;trying to sleep earlier.&quot; This guide synthesizes what published sleep research generally recommends into a simple, week-long plan.

## Why light matters most

Sleep researchers consistently point to two environmental signals as the strongest cues for the circadian system: **bright light in the morning** and **the absence of bright light at night**. Compared to these, factors like caffeine timing and room temperature are smaller levers — useful, but secondary.

## The seven-day plan

### Days 1–2: Anchor your wake time

Pick a wake time you can hit every day for a week — including weekends. Within 10 minutes of waking, expose your eyes to bright light for 10–15 minutes. Outside is best (even on cloudy days, daylight is far brighter than indoor light). A sunrise alarm or daylight lamp is a solid alternative when the sun isn&apos;t up yet.

### Days 3–4: Build the wind-down

Two hours before your target bedtime, switch off overhead lights and use only warm, low lamps. Cut screen time if you can; if you can&apos;t, lower brightness and use a night-shift / warm-color mode. The goal isn&apos;t perfection — it&apos;s reducing the bright-blue signal that tells your brain &quot;it&apos;s still daytime.&quot;

### Days 5–7: Stabilize and protect

By the end of the week, stick rigidly to the same wake time and the same lights-out window. Avoid weekend lie-ins — they undo the work. If you&apos;re still struggling to fall asleep, your bedtime may simply be too early; let sleep pressure build for an extra 30–45 minutes.

<Callout kind="tip" title="The single most important habit">
  A consistent wake time matters more than a consistent bedtime. Bedtimes are
  the result of a stable circadian rhythm; wake times are the lever you use to
  build one.
</Callout>

## What about supplements?

Melatonin is often used to nudge a sleep schedule, but it works very differently from how most people assume. It&apos;s not a sedative — it&apos;s a darkness signal. When used, the most effective approach in research literature tends to be a **small dose** (0.3–0.5mg, far less than typical OTC products) taken **several hours before** the desired bedtime, not at bedtime itself. Talk to a doctor before starting melatonin, especially if you take other medications.

## When to seek help

<Callout kind="warning" title="See a doctor if…">
  You&apos;ve given consistent sleep hygiene a fair shot and you still
  can&apos;t fall asleep, you wake repeatedly through the night, or your
  daytime function is suffering. Persistent insomnia and conditions like
  delayed sleep phase disorder are well-treated by sleep medicine
  specialists — but they need a real diagnosis, not a blog post.
</Callout>
