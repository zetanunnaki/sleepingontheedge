Your core body temperature has to drop by about 1°C for deep sleep to begin. The fastest way to help that happen is to make the room around you colder than you are.

## The number

**60–67°F (15.5–19.5°C).** Most adults sleep best at the cooler end of that range. If you wake up sweating, you're too hot. If you wake up curled into a ball, you're too cold.

<Callout kind="science" title="The Science">
  Your hypothalamus uses skin temperature, not air temperature, to time melatonin release. Cooling the surface your body touches (mattress, bedding) is up to 4x more effective than cooling the air alone.
</Callout>

## Cooling levers, ranked by impact

1. **Mattress surface temperature.** Bedding traps heat against your skin. A cooled mattress topper beats every other intervention by a wide margin.
2. **Air temperature.** A simple fan or AC at 65°F is enough for most people.
3. **Pre-sleep warm shower.** Counterintuitive but works: a hot shower 90 minutes before bed dilates surface vessels, then dumps heat fast once you're out.

<Callout kind="warning" title="Don't Overdo It">
  Below 60°F, sleep quality degrades for most people. Cold becomes a stressor, raising cortisol and fragmenting REM. The goal is *cool*, not *cold*.
</Callout>
