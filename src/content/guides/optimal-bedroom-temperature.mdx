Your core body temperature naturally drops as you transition into deep sleep. According to most sleep researchers, helping that process along by keeping your bedroom cool is one of the simplest, lowest-effort interventions for sleep quality. This guide summarizes what the published research and expert guidelines suggest about bedroom temperature, plus practical ways to hit the target range.

## The recommended range

Most sleep organizations — including the National Sleep Foundation and the American Academy of Sleep Medicine — point to roughly **60–67°F (15.5–19.5°C)** as the temperature range associated with the best sleep for the average adult. Individual preference varies, but if you wake up sweating or curled into a ball, that&apos;s usually a sign you&apos;re outside the window.

<Callout kind="science" title="Why temperature matters">
  The hypothalamus uses skin temperature, not just air temperature, to time
  melatonin release and the transition into deep sleep. Cooling the surface
  in contact with your skin (mattress, bedding) is therefore one of the most
  direct ways to support that process — sometimes more effective than
  cooling the room air alone.
</Callout>

## Cooling levers, ranked by impact

1. **Mattress and bedding surface temperature.** Heavy bedding traps heat against your skin. A breathable mattress topper or actively cooled pad addresses the variable that matters most for many sleepers.
2. **Air temperature.** A fan or AC at the lower end of the recommended range works for most people and is the cheapest option.
3. **A pre-bed warm shower.** Counterintuitively, warming the body 60–90 minutes before bed can dilate blood vessels and accelerate the body&apos;s natural cool-down once you&apos;re out of the shower — an effect supported by several studies.

<Callout kind="warning" title="Don't go overboard">
  Sleeping in a room that&apos;s genuinely cold (well below 60°F) tends to
  fragment sleep for most people, not improve it. The goal is <em>cool</em>,
  not <em>cold</em>.
</Callout>
