If you regularly wake up at the same time in the middle of the night and can&apos;t get back to sleep, you&apos;re not imagining it. Middle-of-the-night insomnia (sleep researchers call it *sleep maintenance insomnia*) is one of the most common sleep complaints — and it usually has a small, addressable set of causes. This guide summarizes what published research and clinical guidelines suggest is happening, and what to try first.

## What&apos;s happening at 3 AM

Sleep is structured in roughly 90-minute cycles. As the night progresses, the proportion of REM in each cycle increases, while deep (slow-wave) sleep decreases. By the second half of the night, you&apos;re in lighter stages and naturally closer to the wake threshold — which means smaller disruptions can tip you over.

Cortisol, the body&apos;s primary stress hormone, also follows a circadian curve: it bottoms out around midnight, then begins rising in the early morning hours to prepare you for waking. That rise can start as early as 2–4 AM. In a relaxed nervous system, you don&apos;t notice it. In an anxious or activated system, it can push you over the wake threshold.

<Callout kind="science" title="This is normal — to a point">
  Healthy adults typically have several brief awakenings per night that
  they don&apos;t remember. The problem isn&apos;t waking — it&apos;s
  staying awake.
</Callout>

## The most common causes

### Stress and an activated nervous system

By far the most common driver. Anxiety, work stress, and unresolved mental load activate the sympathetic nervous system, which makes the morning cortisol rise more pronounced and harder to sleep through.

### Alcohol earlier in the evening

Alcohol helps many people fall asleep but is consistently shown in research to fragment sleep in the second half of the night, often producing rebound wakefulness around 2–4 AM as it metabolizes.

### Caffeine eaten too late in the day

Even if you fall asleep on time, caffeine&apos;s long half-life can affect sleep architecture and make middle-of-the-night wake-ups more likely.

### A late or large meal

Heavy meals close to bedtime can elevate body temperature, trigger reflux, and disrupt sleep continuity.

### Bedroom environment

Light leaks, noise, or temperature fluctuations (a heater kicking on, your partner moving) can act as the trigger that turns a brief, normal awakening into a fully awake state.

### A partner&apos;s sleep apnea

Snoring or breathing pauses from a bed partner is a surprisingly common cause people overlook. If your partner snores loudly and you wake at the same time most nights, that&apos;s worth investigating.

### Untreated sleep apnea (yours)

If you snore, gasp, or feel unrefreshed despite a full night in bed, sleep apnea is a possibility. It&apos;s a medical condition with serious cardiovascular implications and is well-treated when diagnosed.

## What to try first

### Tonight

- Turn the room cooler — many people sleep through 3 AM better at 65°F than at 70°F.
- Block stray light. Even a small amount can prevent you from dropping back into deeper stages.
- Skip any alcohol, especially after 7 PM.

### This week

- Move your last caffeine earlier (use the <a href="/tools/caffeine-cutoff-calculator">caffeine cutoff calculator</a>).
- Try a brief 10-minute wind-down ritual to lower your nervous system before bed.
- Don&apos;t look at the clock when you wake. Knowing the exact time tends to make the problem worse.

### If you wake and can&apos;t fall back asleep

<Callout kind="tip" title="The 20-minute rule">
  If you&apos;re still awake after about 20 minutes, get out of bed. Go to
  another room, sit in dim light, and read something boring. Return to bed
  only when you feel sleepy. Lying awake in bed for hours teaches your brain
  to associate the bed with wakefulness — the opposite of what you want.
</Callout>

## When to see a doctor

<Callout kind="warning" title="Talk to a healthcare provider if…">
  You wake repeatedly every night for more than two to three weeks, you
  snore loudly or have been told you stop breathing in your sleep, or your
  daytime function is significantly impaired. Insomnia is well-treated by
  CBT-I (cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia), which sleep medicine
  specialists consider the first-line approach.
</Callout>
