There&apos;s no shortage of sleep advice on the internet — but a lot of it is recycled, contradictory, or wishful thinking. This list focuses on the changes that show up over and over in published sleep research and clinical guidelines, ordered roughly by how much impact most adults can expect from them. Don&apos;t try all twelve at once. Pick the highest-impact one that&apos;s missing from your routine and start there.

## The big three (do these first)

### 1. Anchor your wake time

A consistent wake time — even on weekends — is the single most reliable lever for stabilizing your circadian rhythm. Most sleep researchers consider it more important than a consistent bedtime, because bedtimes are the *result* of a stable rhythm, not the cause.

### 2. Get bright light in your eyes within 30 minutes of waking

Light is the strongest signal your body uses to set its internal clock. Aim for at least 10 minutes of bright outdoor light (or a daylight lamp / sunrise alarm in winter). This single habit shifts the entire downstream cascade — including when you&apos;ll feel sleepy that night.

### 3. Cut bright overhead light 2 hours before bed

In the evening, switch off ceiling lights and use warm, low lamps. The goal is to lower the &quot;daytime&quot; signal your retina is sending to your hypothalamus. This is more important than blocking your phone screen entirely, though doing both helps.

<Callout kind="science" title="Why these three matter most">
  Light is the dominant <em>zeitgeber</em> (time-giver) for the human
  circadian system — far more powerful than meal timing, exercise, or
  social cues. Get the light right and most of the rest gets easier.
</Callout>

## The next layer

### 4. Stop caffeine 8–10 hours before bed

Caffeine has a roughly 5-hour half-life. That afternoon coffee is still ~25% active at bedtime — and may suppress deep sleep even if you fall asleep on time. See our <a href="/tools/caffeine-cutoff-calculator">caffeine cutoff calculator</a> for a personalized estimate.

### 5. Cool your bedroom

Most sleep organizations recommend a bedroom in the 60–67°F range. Cooler air supports the natural drop in core body temperature that your brain associates with deep sleep onset.

### 6. Block light in your bedroom

Even small amounts of light hitting your retina at night can suppress melatonin and fragment sleep architecture. Blackout curtains or a quality eye mask are cheap, high-leverage fixes.

### 7. Keep screens out of bed

Not because of blue light — that&apos;s a smaller factor than the marketing implies — but because of *content*. Your phone is engineered to keep you awake. Charge it in another room.

## The fine-tuning layer

### 8. Limit alcohol within 3 hours of bed

Alcohol can help you *fall* asleep but consistently shows up in research as a fragmenter of sleep architecture. It particularly suppresses REM in the first half of the night.

### 9. Eat your last large meal 2–3 hours before bed

Large, fatty, or spicy meals close to bedtime are associated with reduced sleep quality and reflux symptoms in many people. A small snack is fine.

### 10. Move during the day — but not too late

Daytime exercise consistently improves sleep quality across studies. Vigorous exercise within an hour or two of bed can be activating for some people, though others tolerate it fine.

### 11. Use the bedroom for sleep

The behavioral principle is called *stimulus control*: the more your bedroom is associated with non-sleep activities (work, scrolling, eating), the harder it becomes to fall asleep there. Make the bed for sleep and intimacy.

### 12. Get out of bed if you can&apos;t sleep within ~20 minutes

Lying awake reinforces the association between &quot;in bed&quot; and &quot;awake.&quot; Get up, do something quiet and dim, and return to bed when you feel sleepy. This is a core technique in CBT-I, the gold-standard treatment for insomnia.

## When tips aren&apos;t enough

<Callout kind="warning" title="See a sleep specialist if…">
  You&apos;ve given consistent sleep hygiene a fair shot for several weeks
  and you still can&apos;t fall asleep, you wake repeatedly through the
  night, you snore loudly or stop breathing in your sleep, or your daytime
  function is suffering. Conditions like insomnia, sleep apnea, and delayed
  sleep phase disorder are well-treated by sleep medicine specialists.
</Callout>
