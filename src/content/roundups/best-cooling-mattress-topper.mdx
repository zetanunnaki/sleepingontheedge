Hot sleepers don't usually need a whole new mattress — they need a cooling layer between themselves and the hot foam they already own. A good cooling topper is the single most cost-effective fix for memory foam mattresses that retain too much heat, and it can also dramatically improve the breathability of firm innerspring beds. **Natural latex and gel-infused memory foam** are the two topper categories that consistently perform best for cooling in aggregated reviews — for very different reasons.

This guide covers both winners with clear guidance on which is right for you.

## Why mattresses sleep hot

The physics are simple: closed-cell memory foam traps body heat against your skin. It conforms beautifully, but the same structure that creates the cradling sensation also prevents air from flowing through the mattress. Heat generated by your body accumulates at the sleeping surface and has nowhere to go.

There are three ways to fix this:

1. **Replace the mattress** with a cooler material (latex, hybrid, innerspring) — expensive.
2. **Add active cooling** like the BedJet 3 — moderately expensive.
3. **Add a passive cooling topper** — cheapest.

A cooling topper creates a more breathable layer at the surface, which lets body heat dissipate instead of getting trapped. Combined with breathable sheets, it often solves the problem for under $200.

## What makes a topper "cooling"

**1. Material breathability.** Natural latex has thousands of tiny open cells that air can flow through. Memory foam is closed-cell and traps heat. Gel infusion adds thermal mass but doesn't change the closed-cell structure.

**2. Moisture wicking.** Latex and open-cell foams pull moisture away from the skin, which accelerates evaporative cooling.

**3. Thermal conductivity.** Gel infusion absorbs heat briefly before becoming saturated. Latex dissipates heat continuously. This is why gel "feels cool on contact" but latex "sleeps cool all night."

**4. Thickness.** Thicker toppers conform more but also trap more heat. 2–3 inches is the sweet spot for cooling — thick enough to soften the mattress but not so thick that it creates its own heat pocket.

## How to maximize cooling beyond the topper

**Pair with breathable sheets.** A cooling topper under heavy flannel sheets is self-defeating. Use bamboo viscose, eucalyptus (Tencel), or percale cotton sheets for maximum airflow to the topper.

**Lower the room temperature.** Cooling toppers work by transferring heat to the surrounding air. If the air is 80°F, the topper has nowhere to dissipate heat. Aim for a 60–68°F bedroom.

**Use a fan for evaporative cooling.** Fans accelerate moisture evaporation from your skin, which your cooling topper supports by letting that moisture wick away from the mattress.

**Consider a cooled pillow too.** The head area runs especially hot because of blood flow to the brain. A cooling pillow (or just a thinner cool pillow) rounds out the cooling system.
