If you've ever bought melatonin at a drugstore, you've probably noticed something weird: the shelves are full of 3 mg, 5 mg, and 10 mg bottles. Then you read the published sleep research and find that most controlled trials used **0.3 mg to 1 mg** — and found that higher doses were either no better or actively worse for sleep quality. There's a disconnect between what's marketed and what the evidence supports.

This guide covers the low-dose melatonin that consistently leads aggregated reviews from people who learned this lesson the hard way (usually after waking up groggy on 5 mg for a week).

## Why low-dose melatonin is the right starting point

**1. Physiological dose.** Your body produces roughly 0.1–0.3 mg of melatonin naturally at night. A 5 mg supplement is roughly 25–50x that amount — a pharmacological dose, not a physiological one. Most published sleep research uses 0.3–1 mg specifically to stay within the physiological range.

**2. Next-day grogginess.** The #1 complaint about high-dose melatonin in aggregated reviews is waking up feeling hungover. This is dose-dependent — it gets significantly worse at 5+ mg. Lower doses avoid it.

**3. Diminishing returns at higher doses.** Multiple published trials have compared 0.3 mg, 1 mg, and 3+ mg doses for sleep onset. The results consistently show that 0.3–1 mg is as effective as higher doses for sleep onset in most people, without the grogginess.

**4. Easier to titrate.** Starting low lets you find the minimum effective dose for you. Starting at 5 mg and working down is harder.

## What to look for on a melatonin label

**1. Dose per tablet.** Look for 1 mg, 0.5 mg, or 0.3 mg options. Many drugstore brands only sell 3 mg and up.

**2. Form.** Fast-dissolve tablets release melatonin quickly, which aligns with the window when it's useful. Time-release tablets keep melatonin elevated through the night, which is theoretically appealing but not well-supported for most sleep users.

**3. No proprietary blends.** Skip products that combine melatonin with valerian, GABA, 5-HTP, and random herbs. You want to know what you're taking, not a mystery cocktail.

**4. Third-party testing.** Melatonin supplements have a notorious accuracy problem — studies have found dosing ranging from 17% to 478% of label claims across the category. Look for USP, NSF, or ConsumerLab verification.

## How to actually use melatonin

The biggest misconception about melatonin is that it's a sleeping pill. It's not — it's a **timing signal**. It tells your body "it's dark, it's time to start the sleep cascade." It doesn't knock you out the way an antihistamine or Ambien does.

This has two implications:

**1. Take it at the right time.** The standard recommendation is 30–60 minutes before target bedtime. Taking it an hour earlier than usual is how you *shift* your sleep schedule — which is the legitimate jet lag use.

**2. Don't take it with dinner or a bright room.** Bright light after taking melatonin blunts its effect significantly. Take it in dim light, 30–60 minutes before you want to be asleep.

**3. Don't expect a knockout.** If you take 1 mg and lie down in a bright room with your phone, you won't feel much. The melatonin provides a timing signal; the dark quiet environment provides the conditions.

## When melatonin actually helps

**Jet lag.** The single best-supported use case. Taking 0.3–1 mg in the evening at the destination time zone for 3–5 nights after arrival consistently speeds circadian adjustment.

**Delayed sleep phase.** For night owls trying to shift to an earlier bedtime, 0.5–1 mg taken 5–7 hours before current sleep onset (yes, earlier than you'd think) can gradually advance the circadian clock.

**Shift work transitions.** Helps reset the clock after switching between day and night shifts.

**Not great for:** insomnia where the problem is anxious rumination, sleep apnea, or mid-night awakenings. Melatonin helps you fall asleep at a new time; it doesn't help you stay asleep or reduce 3 AM waking.
