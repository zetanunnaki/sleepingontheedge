Earplugs are one of the cheapest, most effective sleep interventions available — and one of the most underused. If ambient noise (traffic, snoring, neighbors, early birds) is fragmenting your sleep, earplugs can reduce it to a level your brain ignores, without the electricity, the app, or the $200 price tag of a sound machine.

This guide covers how noise reduction ratings work, the difference between reusable silicone and disposable foam, comfort considerations for side sleepers, and the two products that consistently top aggregated buyer reviews.

## How NRR ratings work

Every earplug sold in the U.S. carries a **Noise Reduction Rating (NRR)** — a number in decibels that indicates how much sound the earplug blocks under lab conditions. A few things to know:

- **Real-world reduction is lower than the lab number.** The EPA recommends subtracting 7 dB from the NRR, then dividing by 2, to estimate actual protection. So an NRR 29 earplug provides roughly (29−7)/2 = **11 dB of real-world reduction**.
- **11 dB is meaningful.** A 10 dB reduction cuts perceived loudness roughly in half. That&apos;s often enough to drop a snoring partner from &quot;keeping me awake&quot; to &quot;barely noticeable.&quot;
- **You don&apos;t want total silence.** Blocking all sound means you won&apos;t hear a smoke alarm. For sleep, you want to reduce ambient noise enough that it stops fragmenting your sleep — not eliminate all sound.

## Reusable silicone vs. disposable foam

### Reusable silicone (Loop, Flare, etc.)

- **Pros:** Washable, durable, consistent fit, more comfortable for many side sleepers because the profile is flatter
- **Cons:** Lower NRR (typically 22–27 dB), higher upfront cost ($20–$35), some designs don&apos;t seal well in all ear canals
- **Best for:** Nightly use, side sleepers, partner snoring, moderate noise environments

### Disposable foam (Mack&apos;s, Hearos, etc.)

- **Pros:** Highest NRR (up to 33 dB), extremely cheap per pair, conform to any ear canal shape
- **Cons:** Single-use (though many people reuse for a few nights), can feel warm, must be rolled and inserted correctly for full seal
- **Best for:** Heavy noise environments, travel, trying earplugs for the first time (cheapest way to test)

## 1. Loop Quiet — Best Reusable

Loop is the most-recommended reusable sleep earplug in aggregated buyer reviews. The design uses a hollow acoustic channel inside a silicone shell that reduces noise without the pressure and heat of foam.

### What buyers like

- **Side-sleeper comfort.** The flush, flat profile means the earplug doesn&apos;t press into the pillow. This is the #1 reason side sleepers choose Loop over foam.
- **26 dB NRR.** Enough to cut snoring, traffic, and early-morning noise to manageable levels without total isolation.
- **Reusable and washable.** One pair lasts months with proper care. Multiple ear tip sizes are included for fit.
- **Aesthetics.** They look good enough to wear outside sleep contexts — concerts, focus work, travel.

### What buyers complain about

- **Fit varies.** Some ear canal shapes don&apos;t seal well with the included tips. Loop sells supplemental tips, but it can take experimentation.
- **Not enough for heavy noise.** If you live next to a highway or your partner&apos;s snoring is severe, 26 dB may not be sufficient.

Mack&apos;s is the best-selling earplug brand on Amazon, and the Slim Fit model is designed specifically for people with smaller ear canals — a common pain point with standard-size foam plugs.

### What buyers like

- **29 dB NRR.** Among the highest available, strong enough for heavy snoring and urban noise.
- **Slim profile.** Specifically designed for smaller ears — women and people who find standard foam plugs uncomfortable consistently rate these higher.
- **Price.** At $13 for a 50-pair box, they cost pennies per night.

### What buyers complain about

- **Disposable.** Not ideal for the environmentally conscious. Many users reuse each pair for 3–5 nights, but they&apos;re not designed for it.
- **Insertion technique matters.** Foam earplugs must be rolled tightly and inserted while compressed for a full seal. Buyers who skip this step report poor noise reduction.

## Tips for sleeping with earplugs

<Callout kind="tip" title="Getting the most out of foam earplugs">
  Roll the plug into the thinnest cylinder you can. Pull your ear up and
  back with your opposite hand to straighten the ear canal. Insert the plug
  and hold it for 15–20 seconds while it expands. If you can still hear
  normal conversation clearly, the seal isn&apos;t right — try again.
</Callout>

- **Combine with a sound machine** for the most effective noise management. Earplugs reduce the peaks; a sound machine masks the residual noise.
- **Clean reusable plugs regularly** — warm water and mild soap. Dirty plugs can cause ear infections.
- **Don&apos;t push foam plugs in too deep.** The tip should sit at the entrance of the ear canal, not deep inside it.
- **If your ears feel sore,** you&apos;re using the wrong size. Try a slimmer profile or switch between foam and silicone.

## When earplugs aren&apos;t enough

If you need earplugs because of a partner&apos;s snoring and the snoring includes pauses, gasps, or choking sounds, the real solution is a sleep evaluation for the snorer — not better earplugs for you.
