Magnesium is one of the most-recommended sleep supplements on the internet — but most products on shelves use forms that were never designed for sleep support. This guide covers what the published research suggests about magnesium and sleep, what to look for on a label, and the products that verified buyers consistently rate highest.

## Why magnesium for sleep?

Magnesium is involved in hundreds of biological processes, including the regulation of GABA — a neurotransmitter that helps calm the nervous system. Some studies, particularly in older adults and people with low dietary magnesium, suggest supplementation may modestly improve sleep onset and sleep quality. Results in the general healthy-adult population are more mixed, and supplementation is **not** a substitute for treatment when an underlying sleep disorder is present.

<Callout kind="science" title="What the research shows">
  A 2022 systematic review in <em>BMC Complementary Medicine and Therapies</em>
  found that oral magnesium supplementation was associated with modest
  improvements in subjective sleep quality, particularly in older adults and
  in those with documented insufficiency. Effects in healthy younger adults
  with adequate dietary intake were generally smaller.
</Callout>

## The forms that matter

Not all magnesium is the same. The form determines absorption, side effects, and where in the body it acts.

- **Magnesium glycinate** — bound to the amino acid glycine, which has its own mild calming effect. Generally well tolerated and the form most often recommended in sleep contexts.
- **Magnesium L-threonate** — has shown the ability to cross the blood-brain barrier in animal studies; marketed for cognitive and sleep support, though human evidence is still emerging.
- **Magnesium citrate** — well absorbed but tends to have a laxative effect at higher doses, which is why it&apos;s a poor choice for nightly use.
- **Magnesium oxide** — cheap and widely sold but very poorly absorbed; often used in products you should generally skip if your goal is sleep.

## What to look for on the label

<Callout kind="tip" title="Reading a magnesium label">
  Manufacturers list the elemental magnesium content and the form. A 1,000mg
  &quot;magnesium glycinate&quot; capsule typically contains only ~140mg of
  elemental magnesium — the rest is the glycine. Compare elemental amounts,
  not total compound weights.
</Callout>

The U.S. RDA for magnesium is roughly 310–420mg per day depending on age and sex, including dietary intake. Most people don&apos;t need a megadose to see benefit, and going over the upper limit (350mg/day from supplements alone, per the NIH) can cause GI side effects.
