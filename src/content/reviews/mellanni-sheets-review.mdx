The term "microfiber" means very fine polyester threads, typically less than 1 denier in diameter. Brushing the surface creates a soft, slightly fuzzy texture. The result is a fabric that feels surprisingly soft for synthetic material, resists wrinkles aggressively, and costs very little to manufacture. The trade-off — and there is one — is breathability.

## What buyers consistently complain about

### 1. Traps heat

The #1 complaint, and it&apos;s a fundamental limitation of polyester. Microfiber doesn&apos;t breathe — it doesn&apos;t wick moisture, doesn&apos;t allow airflow, and doesn&apos;t dissipate body heat. Hot sleepers, sleepers in warm climates, and anyone who sweats at night consistently report waking up warm and clammy.

This isn&apos;t a defect — it&apos;s the physics of synthetic fabric. If you sleep hot, microfiber is the wrong material at any price point. Bamboo viscose or eucalyptus lyocell are the budget-friendly cooling alternatives.

### 2. Less durable than cotton

Microfiber fibers are thin and prone to snagging, tearing, and thinning over time. Long-term reviewers (1+ year) consistently report the fabric becoming noticeably thinner and developing small holes or tears. Plan for a 1–2 year lifespan with regular use — but at $29, annual replacement still costs less than a single set of premium cotton sheets.

### 3. Static cling

Polyester generates static electricity, especially in dry climates or during winter. Multiple buyers report dealing with static cling when getting in and out of bed — a minor annoyance that doesn&apos;t occur with natural fiber sheets.

### 4. Doesn&apos;t feel like cotton or bamboo

Despite the softness, microfiber has a distinctly synthetic texture that some buyers find unappealing once they compare it side-by-side with cotton or bamboo. The brushed surface is soft, but it lacks the "cool crispness" of cotton percale or the "silky drape" of sateen and lyocell. Texture-sensitive sleepers may find microfiber unsatisfying regardless of softness.
